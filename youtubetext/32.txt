Our impact Culture and trends Progress and impact Our impact People all over the world have used YouTube to follow their passions, connect with others, and develop economic opportunities for themselves and their communities. Explore the stories behind just a few of YouTube’s creative entrepreneurs here. YouTube Impact YouTube Impact Stories Every day, YouTube helps people learn new skills, start businesses, create jobs, and enrich their lives. A study by Oxford Economics found that YouTube's creative ecosystem contributed approximately $923 million CAD to the Canadian GDP, equivalent to 34,100 Full Time Equivalent (FTE) jobs in Canada in 2020. 72% of creative entrepreneurs said YouTube has had a positive impact on their professional goals. 86% of users said they use YouTube to gather information and knowledge. 73% of businesses with a YouTube channel agreed that YouTube has helped them grow their customer base. Read more about YouTube's Impact in Canada Download report Mitchell Moffit and Gregory Brown Eight years ago, Greg and Mitch started a YouTube channel to make science make sense. More than 9 million subscribers later, AsapSCIENCE has become one of the largest learning channels globally, with scientific answers to how productivity works, why we fall in love, and more. Aysha Harun At just 16 years old, Aysha began posting beauty and fashion videos on YouTube after noticing there was no one in the industry that looked like her. Nine years later, she’s made a successful career on YouTube as a Black Muslim woman and inspires a widely underrepresented community in the beauty and lifestyle space. Molly Burke Molly was diagnosed with a rare degenerative eye disease at the age of 4 and lost most of her vision by the time she was a teenager, but she didn't let that hold her back. She uses YouTube to share her story on how she adapted to life without sight, and has become a motivational speaker and accessibility advocate. James Hobson James Hobson, an engineer turned YouTube creator, saw an opportunity to inspire viewers with the magic of STEM. He and the Hacksmith team take fictional ideas from movies and comics and turn them into real-life prototypes, from lightsabers to electromagnetic shields. Yolanda Gampp Producers Connie and Jocelyn cast Yolanda Gampp for a reality TV show that ended up getting cancelled, and when they couldn't convince a network to greenlight a new show, they decided to create their own. They took their vision to YouTube and How to Cake It was born. With Yolanda's extraordinary baking talents, and Connie and Jocelyn's experience as content producers and brand strategists, How To Cake It has grown into a global media empire. Sandi Brock Sandi Brock accidentally fell into sheep farming, and has now become part of a growing YouTube community offering candid insights about the agricultural community. Audiences from across the globe tune in to follow her journey as a farmer, and her learnings along the way. Cynthia Dulude A professional makeup artist, Cynthia headed to YouTube to share her tips and tricks on makeup, hairstyle, fashion and style. She's also the author of two books "Le Petit Guide de la Beauté" and "Bien Chez Moi." Émile Roy A skillful director and a talented screenwriter, Émile uses YouTube to share his creative sketches with the world, from short films and drama series to documentaries that show the realities of his generation. Gabrielle Marion Gabrielle is an important voice in Quebec's LGBTQ+ community, and uses her platform on YouTube to share her experiences as a young trans woman, and reflections on transitioning and defining identity. Kelsey MacDermaid and Becky Wright Kelsey and Becky are an entrepreneurial duo who tackle creative makeovers, life hacks and renovations on their YouTube channel. They inspire young women to "do-it-themselves," from making a tool purchase to redesigning an apartment on a budget.
